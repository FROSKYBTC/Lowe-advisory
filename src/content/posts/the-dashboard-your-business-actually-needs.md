I've yet to walk into a business that didn't have *some* kind of dashboard. I've also yet to walk into one where the leadership team actually trusted the dashboard in front of them.

The problem isn't usually the tooling. It's the design. Most dashboards are built bottom-up — "what data do we have?" — instead of top-down: *"what decisions do we need to make, and what would tell us we're making them well?"*

## What most dashboards get wrong

- **Too many metrics.** Twenty numbers is zero numbers. Attention is finite; if everything is highlighted, nothing is.
- **Lagging only.** Revenue and profit tell you what *happened*. They don't tell you what's *about to* happen.
- **No baseline.** A number without context (target, trend, or comparison) is just trivia.
- **Updated too late.** A dashboard you check monthly is a report. A dashboard you check daily changes behavior.

## The five numbers that matter

For most small and mid-sized businesses, a genuinely useful dashboard fits on a single screen with five metrics, each chosen for a reason:

1. **Cash runway.** How many months you can operate at current burn, assuming nothing changes. This is the one number that buys you the right to make every other decision calmly.
2. **Leading indicator of revenue.** Not last month's sales — the thing that *predicts* next month's. Pipeline value, qualified leads, bookings, depending on your model.
3. **Gross margin.** Not revenue. Revenue growth with eroding margin is a treadmill, not growth.
4. **Customer health.** A proxy for retention and satisfaction — NPS, churn, product usage, support volume. Something that warns you *before* revenue dips.
5. **Throughput / delivery capacity.** How much work the business can handle before quality cracks. This tells you when it's safe to push the gas.

Five numbers. Each tied to a decision. Each with a target and a trend.

## How to actually use it

A dashboard's value isn't in looking at it — it's in what changes afterward. The test:

- **Does each metric have an owner?** If a number moves, someone knows *why*, and someone can *do something about it*.
- **Do you leave with actions?** If you stare at the dashboard and then go back to your day unchanged, it's not a dashboard. It's a screensaver.
- **Do you trust it?** If leadership quietly keeps a separate spreadsheet "with the real numbers," the dashboard has already failed. Fix the trust before you fix the tool.

## Start ugly, start now

You don't need a BI platform. The most effective dashboard I've seen was a single shared spreadsheet, updated by hand every Monday, with five cells highlighted yellow. It worked because the *discipline* behind it was real.

Build the smallest version that tells the truth. Use it weekly. Let the questions it raises tell you what to add. In six months you'll have a dashboard that actually steers the business — and more importantly, a leadership team that agrees on what "good" looks like.
